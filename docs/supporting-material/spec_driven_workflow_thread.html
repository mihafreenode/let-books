Writing block: Spec-Driven Development Beyond Software

Spec-Driven Development Beyond Software

Although Spec-Driven Development is often discussed in the context of software engineering, the underlying idea applies to many forms of knowledge work.

A specification is simply a structured description of the desired result, its constraints, and the criteria used to determine whether the result is acceptable.

Academic and Research Writing

A research paper can be guided by specifications such as required citation style, publication formatting requirements, target audience, expected level of evidence, terminology consistency, language quality standards, and ethical and disclosure requirements.

Educational Content

Educational materials can be developed from specifications that define target age group, prerequisite knowledge, learning objectives, reading difficulty, accessibility requirements, and assessment criteria.

Legal and Regulatory Documents

Legal writing already relies heavily on formal specifications. A specification may define jurisdiction, required clauses, approved terminology, regulatory references, and review and approval workflows.

The Role of Validators

Specifications become significantly more useful when accompanied by validators. Validators provide objective checks that determine whether a document satisfies some part of its specification.

Not Every Author Must Build Validators

Creating reliable validators may require software engineering expertise, domain knowledge, infrastructure, and ongoing maintenance. Individual writers, researchers, educators, and legal professionals should not necessarily be expected to build these tools themselves.

AI and Specifications

AI systems can assist both content creation and validation. However, specifications remain valuable because they make expectations explicit, allow work to be reviewed objectively, and provide a durable standard that survives changes in authors, tools, and AI models.

Writing block: Validators as Long-Term Assets

Validators as Long-Term Assets

Specifications describe expectations. Validators make those expectations executable.

The immediate purpose of a validator is to help determine whether a piece of work satisfies some aspect of its specification. Over time, however, validators often become useful beyond the original project that motivated their creation.

A language-quality validator developed for one publication may later be reused across many publications. A citation validator may help multiple research groups follow the same publishing standards. Accessibility, compliance, and style validators can provide consistent feedback to authors, reviewers, and editors across an entire organization.

This is similar to how automated tests in software often outlive the feature that originally justified them. Once established, validators can help preserve institutional knowledge, reduce review effort, and promote consistency even as authors, tools, and workflows change.

Not every validator needs to be developed by individual authors. In many cases, institutions, publishers, universities, professional organizations, or regulatory bodies are better positioned to provide shared validation tools that reflect their standards and requirements.

As AI-assisted content creation becomes more common, reusable validators may play an increasingly important role by providing objective checks that remain stable even as underlying authoring tools evolve.

Writing block: Starting with Interactive AI

Starting with Interactive AI

Specification-Driven Development does not require coding agents, terminal tools, or software projects.

Many people will first encounter the ideas through interactive AI systems such as ChatGPT, Claude, Gemini, or similar web-based assistants. These tools are often sufficient for drafting specifications, reviewing work, refining requirements, and iterating on complex documents.

A practical workflow is surprisingly simple: define the objective and constraints; ask the AI to produce a draft; review and refine the specification; generate revised content; save both the specification and resulting work; continue future iterations from the saved context.

Why Save Specifications as Markdown?

A practical approach is to save specifications, notes, reviews, and supporting material as Markdown files.

Markdown is human-readable without specialized software, easy to version, supported by many platforms, easily processed by AI systems, and durable. Most importantly, Markdown preserves context in a form that remains useful to both humans and machines.

Preserving More Than the Final Result

Traditional workflows often preserve only the final document. A specification-driven workflow can additionally preserve goals, assumptions, constraints, quality standards, review findings, publication requirements, validation results, and important decisions and their rationale.

Writing block: When Interactive AI Reaches Its Limits

When Interactive AI Reaches Its Limits

Interactive AI systems are often sufficient for drafting documents, exploring ideas, reviewing content, and completing one-time tasks. For many individuals, these capabilities may be all that is needed.

However, larger projects frequently expose practical limitations that become more noticeable as the amount of information, supporting material, and validation requirements grows.

Context Is Finite

Modern AI systems can process far more information than earlier generations, but context remains a finite resource. As projects grow, specifications, source materials, review findings, validation reports, references, and supporting documents may exceed what is practical to keep continuously available within a single conversation.

One response is to store information in durable artifacts such as Markdown documents and allow AI systems to retrieve relevant portions when needed. The goal is not necessarily to provide an AI system with more information at once, but to provide the right information when it is needed.

Tools Are Purposefully Limited

Interactive AI platforms typically provide a curated set of tools that are designed to work safely and reliably for a broad audience. Specialized workflows often require capabilities specific to a profession, organization, or project.

An Evolving Boundary

The distinction between interactive AI systems and agentic environments continues to evolve. What remains relatively constant is the underlying requirement: larger and more complex projects benefit from the ability to retrieve relevant context, use specialized tools, execute validation workflows, and preserve knowledge across many iterations of work.

Writing block: Trust, Security, and Tool Governance

Trust, Security, and Tool Governance

As AI systems gain the ability to use tools, workflows, skills, and external services, security considerations become increasingly important.

Traditional software security focuses primarily on executable code. In AI-assisted systems, the boundary is less clear. Not only programs, scripts, and binaries may influence behavior. Textual instructions, workflow definitions, specifications, prompts, and skill descriptions can also affect how an AI system operates.

In practice, this means that some forms of text may be treated as instructions rather than merely as information.

Prefer Trusted and Curated Skills

Organizations may reasonably prefer internally developed skills, institutionally reviewed workflows, tools provided by trusted vendors, and audited integrations with known maintenance processes.

Examples of organizations that provide extensive ecosystems of tools and integrations include OpenAI, Microsoft, Oracle, and many other enterprise software vendors.

Keep Authorization Outside the AI

A useful design principle is to separate authorization decisions from AI reasoning. AI systems may decide which action appears appropriate, but the actual authorization to perform that action should ideally be enforced by external systems designed specifically for authentication, authorization, auditing, and credential management.

AI Gateways and Institutional Control

Many organizations may choose to introduce AI through controlled gateways rather than granting direct access to every system. An AI gateway can provide centralized authentication, authorization enforcement, auditing and logging, approved tool access, data-loss prevention controls, usage monitoring, and policy enforcement.

Writing block: Iterative Workflows and Human Feedback

Iterative Workflows and Human Feedback

Real-world AI-assisted workflows are rarely linear. Specifications are seldom perfect on the first attempt. Requirements evolve, assumptions are challenged, validation uncovers problems, and new information becomes available as work progresses.

A common pattern looks like this: define an initial objective; create a draft specification; generate a draft result; review the result; refine the specification; regenerate or revise the result; validate the outcome; repeat as necessary.

The Role of the Human Participant

AI systems can assist with drafting, analysis, validation, and automation, but human participants remain responsible for defining goals, evaluating trade-offs, and determining whether results are acceptable.

Using Interactive AI as a Thinking Partner

Even when agentic workflows are available, interactive AI systems can remain useful. Users frequently employ web-based AI assistants to review specifications, critique plans, identify missing requirements, propose validation strategies, generate improved prompts, explain agent feedback, and suggest next steps.

Feedback Is Part of the Specification

Each review comment, validation result, correction, or rejected draft provides additional information about the desired outcome. Over time, recurring feedback often becomes formalized as updated specifications, validation rules, reusable skills, workflow templates, and organizational standards.

Writing block: Localize Examples for the Reader

Localize Examples for the Reader

Examples are often more effective when presented in the reader's primary language.

When translating or localizing documentation, specifications, educational materials, or workflow examples, consider localizing example text as well rather than preserving the original language unnecessarily.

Exceptions may include product names, company names, standards and specifications, programming languages, protocol names, commands, code samples, and other terms commonly used in their original form.

The goal is not linguistic purity. The goal is reducing unnecessary cognitive load for readers.

Localizing Workflow Examples

This principle also applies to conversational examples. If a workflow example contains exchanges such as "I want to extend doc to provide some practical insight...", a localized version may instead present an equivalent example in the target language.
